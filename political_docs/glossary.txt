POLITICAL TERMS GLOSSARY

Filibuster: A tactic used in the U.S. Senate where a senator speaks for an extended period to delay or block a vote on legislation. It requires 60 votes to end.

Cloture: The procedure used to end a filibuster. Requires 60 out of 100 senators to vote in favor.

Bipartisan: When members of both the Democratic and Republican parties work together and agree on legislation.

Veto: The power of the President to reject a bill passed by Congress. Congress can override a veto with a two-thirds majority in both chambers.

Electoral College: The system used to elect the U.S. President. Each state gets a number of electors equal to its total congressional representation. A candidate needs 270 of 538 electoral votes to win.

Gerrymandering: The manipulation of electoral district boundaries to give one political party an advantage over another.

Appropriations: The congressional process of allocating federal funds for specific government programs and agencies.

Reconciliation: A special Senate procedure that allows budget-related legislation to pass with a simple majority (51 votes) instead of the usual 60 needed to overcome a filibuster.

Lame Duck: A period after an election and before a new officeholder takes over, during which the outgoing official has reduced political influence.

Caucus: A meeting of members of a political party to discuss policy or strategy, or a group of legislators with shared interests.
