HOW THE U.S. CONGRESS WORKS

What is Congress?
Congress is the legislative branch of the U.S. government. It is bicameral, meaning it has two chambers: the Senate and the House of Representatives.

The Senate:
- 100 senators total — 2 per state regardless of population
- Senators serve 6-year terms
- One-third of Senate seats are up for election every 2 years
- The Vice President serves as President of the Senate and casts tie-breaking votes
- Special powers: confirms presidential appointments, ratifies treaties, conducts impeachment trials

The House of Representatives:
- 435 representatives total — allocated by state population
- Representatives serve 2-year terms — all seats up for election every 2 years
- The Speaker of the House leads the chamber
- Special powers: originates revenue bills, initiates impeachment proceedings

How do they work together?
Both chambers must pass identical versions of a bill before it goes to the President. This design was intentional — the Founders wanted legislation to be difficult to pass, requiring broad agreement.

What is a congressional session?
Each Congress lasts two years, divided into two one-year sessions. The current Congress is the 119th.

How are committees important?
Most legislative work happens in committees, not the full chamber floor. Committees specialize in areas like finance, foreign affairs, or judiciary. Committee chairs hold significant power over which bills get considered.
