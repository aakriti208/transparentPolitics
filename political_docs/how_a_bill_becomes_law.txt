HOW A BILL BECOMES LAW

Step 1 - Introduction
Any member of Congress can introduce a bill. In the House, the member drops it in "the hopper" (a wooden box). In the Senate, the bill is introduced from the floor.

Step 2 - Committee Review
The bill is assigned to a relevant committee. The committee studies the bill, holds hearings, and can approve, amend, or kill it. Most bills die in committee.

Step 3 - Floor Debate and Vote
If the committee approves, the full chamber debates the bill. Members can propose amendments. The House uses a simple majority (218 of 435). The Senate typically needs 60 votes to overcome a filibuster.

Step 4 - The Other Chamber
The bill must pass both the House and Senate. If the other chamber makes changes, both chambers form a Conference Committee to negotiate a final version.

Step 5 - Presidential Action
The President has four options:
- Sign the bill into law
- Veto the bill and send it back to Congress
- Do nothing for 10 days while Congress is in session (bill becomes law automatically)
- Do nothing for 10 days while Congress is not in session (pocket veto — bill dies)

Step 6 - Veto Override
Congress can override a presidential veto with a two-thirds majority vote in both the House and Senate.
